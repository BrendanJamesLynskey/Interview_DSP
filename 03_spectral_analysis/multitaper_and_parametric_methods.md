## Intermediate

### Q1. What is the multitaper method (Thomson's method) and how does it improve over the standard periodogram?

**Answer:**

**Thomson's multitaper method** (Thomson, 1982) improves the variance of spectral estimates by averaging periodograms computed with multiple orthogonal "tapers" (window functions), rather than a single window.

**The problem with the periodogram:**

The periodogram estimate $\hat{S}(f) = |X(f)|^2 / N$ where $X(f) = \sum_n x[n] e^{-j2\pi fn}$ has:

1. **High variance.** The variance does not decrease with longer signals — it stays roughly equal to the true power. The estimate is "noisy".

2. **Bias from windowing.** Whatever window is used (rectangular, Hann, etc.) determines the spectral leakage characteristics.

The single-window periodogram is a poor estimator for any but the most ideal signals.

**The multitaper idea:**

Instead of one window, use $K$ orthogonal windows $w_k[n]$ for $k = 0, 1, ..., K-1$. Compute $K$ periodograms:

$$\hat{S}_k(f) = |X_k(f)|^2 / N \quad \text{where} \quad X_k(f) = \sum_n w_k[n] x[n] e^{-j2\pi fn}$$

The multitaper estimate is the average:

$$\hat{S}_{MT}(f) = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=0}^{K-1} \hat{S}_k(f)$$

**Discrete prolate spheroidal sequences (DPSS):**

The optimal windows are the **DPSS** (also called **Slepian sequences**). They have the property of being maximally concentrated in a specified frequency band — ideal for spectral analysis.

The DPSS for a given length $N$ and bandwidth $W$ (in cycles per sample) form an orthogonal set. Typically the first $K = 2NW - 1$ are useful.

**Why averaging works:**

1. **Reduced variance.** Each periodogram is noisy, but the noise components are approximately independent (since the tapers are orthogonal). Averaging $K$ independent estimates reduces variance by $1/K$.

2. **Each taper captures different information.** The tapers have different shapes, so they "see" the signal in different ways. The combined estimate is more stable.

3. **Bias control.** The DPSS tapers minimise spectral leakage outside their concentration band.

**Trade-off:**

- **More tapers ($K$ larger):** lower variance but reduced spectral resolution (the effective bandwidth is widened).
- **Fewer tapers:** higher variance but better resolution.

Typically choose $K$ based on the time-bandwidth product: $K \approx 2NW$ where $W$ is the desired half-bandwidth.

**Adaptive multitaper:**

A more sophisticated version weights each taper's contribution adaptively based on the local signal-to-noise ratio. Reduces bias for spectra with sharp peaks.

**Use cases:**

1. **Climate science:** analysing time series of temperature, sea level, etc., where the data is short and noisy.

2. **Neuroscience:** EEG/MEG analysis. Multitaper is the standard for spectral analysis of brain signals.

3. **Astrophysics:** analysing pulsar timing or stellar variability.

4. **Geophysics:** seismic and gravitational wave analysis.

5. **Speech and audio:** wherever robust spectral estimates are needed.

**Compared to other methods:**

- **vs Welch's method:** Welch averages periodograms over overlapping segments of the same signal. Multitaper averages periodograms over the same segment with different tapers. Welch is simpler; multitaper is more efficient (uses all the data, doesn't sacrifice it to overlap).

- **vs AR modeling:** AR modeling fits a parametric model. Better resolution for narrow peaks; worse for broadband signals. Multitaper is non-parametric — fewer assumptions.

**Implementation:**

Most signal processing libraries provide multitaper:
- **Python:** `scipy.signal.windows.dpss`, plus `multitaper_psd` in some packages.
- **MATLAB:** `pmtm()`.
- **R:** `multitaper` package.

**Interview insight:** Multitaper is the "professional" choice for noisy spectral estimation. Knowing about DPSS tapers and the variance/resolution trade-off shows real spectral analysis depth.

### Q2. What is autoregressive (AR) spectral estimation, and how does it compare to FFT-based methods?

**Answer:**

**Autoregressive (AR) spectral estimation** fits a parametric model — an AR filter — to the data and computes the spectrum from the model parameters. It's a parametric alternative to FFT-based methods.

**The AR model:**

An AR(p) model assumes the signal is generated by:

$$x[n] = -\sum_{k=1}^p a_k x[n-k] + e[n]$$

where $e[n]$ is white noise. The current sample is a linear combination of $p$ past samples plus noise.

**The spectrum from the model:**

The transfer function from noise to signal is:

$$H(z) = \frac{1}{1 + \sum_{k=1}^p a_k z^{-k}}$$

The power spectrum is:

$$S(f) = \frac{\sigma_e^2}{|1 + \sum_{k=1}^p a_k e^{-j2\pi fk}|^2}$$

where $\sigma_e^2$ is the noise variance.

**Estimating the AR parameters:**

Several methods exist:

1. **Yule-Walker equations:** solve a linear system based on autocorrelations. Always gives stable filters (poles inside unit circle) but biased for small samples.

2. **Burg's method:** minimises forward and backward prediction errors. Better for short data, always stable.

3. **Covariance method:** least-squares on the prediction error. May give unstable filters.

4. **Modified covariance:** combines forward and backward prediction. Most accurate for sinusoids in noise.

**Order selection:**

The AR order $p$ controls the resolution and complexity:

- Too low: cannot capture all spectral peaks. Underfit.
- Too high: fits noise as if it were signal. Overfit, false peaks.

Selection criteria:
- **AIC (Akaike Information Criterion):** balances fit and model complexity.
- **BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion):** more aggressive penalty for high orders.
- **MDL (Minimum Description Length):** similar.

For a signal with $K$ true sinusoids, $p \approx 2K$ is a good starting point.

**Advantages over FFT methods:**

1. **Better resolution for short data.** AR can resolve closely-spaced sinusoids that the FFT can't, especially with limited samples. The "Rayleigh resolution" (1/N) doesn't apply.

2. **Smooth, peaky output.** The spectrum is computed from a continuous formula, so peaks are smooth. No leakage artifacts.

3. **Natural for narrow-band signals.** A few sinusoids in noise are well-modeled by an AR process with a few resonances.

**Disadvantages:**

1. **Model assumption.** AR assumes the signal really is AR. For broadband signals (multiple distinct frequency components, noise floor variations), AR struggles.

2. **Order selection is tricky.** Wrong order gives bad results. Automated selection isn't always reliable.

3. **Computationally more expensive than FFT** for long data.

4. **Less intuitive.** FFT spectra are familiar; AR spectra require interpretation.

**Use cases:**

- **Speech analysis (linear predictive coding).** Speech is well-modeled as AR with poles representing vocal tract resonances. LPC is the AR-based representation used in cellular voice codecs.

- **Radar signal processing.** Detecting closely-spaced targets.

- **Geophysical signal analysis.** Earthquake signal modeling.

- **Music analysis.** Detecting harmonics with high precision.

**MA and ARMA:**

- **MA (Moving Average):** signal is sum of past noise samples. Models nulls in the spectrum (anti-resonances).
- **ARMA:** combines AR and MA. More flexible but harder to estimate.

For most applications, AR alone is sufficient.

**Comparison summary:**

| Method | Best for | Worst for |
|---|---|---|
| FFT periodogram | Long data, broadband | Short data, sharp peaks |
| Welch's method | Long data with stationarity | Non-stationary signals |
| Multitaper | Noisy, short data | Real-time |
| AR | Few sharp peaks | Broadband, high noise |
| ARMA | Mixed peak/null structure | Computationally bound |

**Interview insight:** A candidate who can compare parametric (AR) and non-parametric (FFT, multitaper) methods, and explain when each is preferred, shows broad spectral analysis knowledge.

## Advanced

### Q3. What is the MUSIC algorithm, and how does it achieve super-resolution?

**Answer:**

**MUSIC (Multiple Signal Classification)** is a high-resolution spectral estimation method introduced by Schmidt (1979, formalised 1986). It can resolve frequency components much closer together than the FFT can — sometimes by an order of magnitude — under appropriate conditions.

**The setup:**

Assume the signal is a sum of $K$ complex sinusoids in white Gaussian noise:

$$x[n] = \sum_{k=1}^K A_k e^{j2\pi f_k n + \phi_k} + w[n]$$

The goal: estimate the frequencies $f_1, ..., f_K$.

**The autocorrelation matrix:**

Form vectors $\mathbf{x}[n] = [x[n], x[n+1], ..., x[n+M-1]]^T$ for some $M > K$.

The autocorrelation matrix is:

$$R = E[\mathbf{x}\mathbf{x}^H]$$

For $K$ sinusoids in noise:

$$R = AS A^H + \sigma^2 I$$

where $A$ is the matrix of sinusoidal "steering vectors" and $S$ is the signal power matrix.

**Eigendecomposition:**

The matrix $R$ has $M$ eigenvalues. They split into two groups:

- **Signal eigenvalues** ($K$ largest): correspond to the $K$ sinusoids.
- **Noise eigenvalues** ($M - K$): all approximately equal to $\sigma^2$ (the noise floor).

The eigenvectors split similarly:

- **Signal subspace** (spanned by $K$ eigenvectors): contains the sinusoids.
- **Noise subspace** (spanned by $M - K$ eigenvectors): orthogonal to the sinusoids.

**The MUSIC pseudospectrum:**

Define a "steering vector" function $\mathbf{a}(f) = [1, e^{j2\pi f}, e^{j4\pi f}, ..., e^{j2\pi(M-1)f}]^T$.

The MUSIC pseudospectrum is:

$$P_{MUSIC}(f) = \frac{1}{\sum_{i=K+1}^M |\mathbf{a}^H(f) \mathbf{u}_i|^2}$$

where $\mathbf{u}_{K+1}, ..., \mathbf{u}_M$ are the noise eigenvectors.

**Why this works:**

When $f$ matches one of the true sinusoidal frequencies, $\mathbf{a}(f)$ lies in the signal subspace, which is orthogonal to the noise subspace. So $\mathbf{a}^H(f) \mathbf{u}_i = 0$ for all noise eigenvectors. The denominator approaches zero, and $P_{MUSIC}(f)$ shoots to infinity (a peak).

When $f$ doesn't match a true frequency, the projections are non-zero, and $P_{MUSIC}(f)$ is moderate.

**Result:** The pseudospectrum has very sharp peaks at the true frequencies — much sharper than an FFT spectrum.

**Why "pseudo"-spectrum:**

It's not a true power spectrum. The peak values don't represent actual signal power; they just locate the frequencies. To get amplitudes, you'd need a separate estimation step.

**Resolution:**

MUSIC can resolve sinusoids separated by much less than $1/N$ (the FFT resolution limit) provided:

1. **Enough samples.** More samples = better noise averaging = better resolution.

2. **High SNR.** Low noise = clean signal/noise subspace separation = better resolution.

3. **Correct $K$.** The number of sinusoids must be known or correctly estimated.

In practice, MUSIC can resolve sinusoids 1/10 to 1/100 of the FFT resolution, which is why it's called "super-resolution".

**Limitations:**

1. **Knowledge of $K$.** You must know (or estimate) the number of sinusoids. If $K$ is wrong, the subspace separation is wrong, and the algorithm fails.

2. **Sinusoidal assumption.** Only works for actual sinusoids in noise. Doesn't handle broadband signals or non-stationary spectra.

3. **Complexity.** Eigendecomposition of $M \times M$ matrices is expensive for large $M$.

4. **High SNR required.** Below ~10 dB SNR, performance degrades rapidly.

**Use cases:**

1. **Direction-of-arrival (DOA) estimation in radar and sonar.** Multiple emitters at slightly different bearings.

2. **NMR spectroscopy.** Resolving closely-spaced spectral lines.

3. **Communication signal analysis.** Detecting closely-spaced carriers.

4. **Vibration analysis.** Identifying close natural frequencies in mechanical systems.

**Interview insight:** A candidate who can explain MUSIC by signal/noise subspace separation shows graduate-level spectral analysis knowledge. The eigendecomposition insight is the key.

### Q4. What is ESPRIT, and how does it differ from MUSIC?

**Answer:**

**ESPRIT (Estimation of Signal Parameters via Rotational Invariance Techniques)** is another super-resolution algorithm, introduced by Roy and Kailath (1989). It's similar in spirit to MUSIC but avoids the expensive search over the pseudospectrum.

**The key insight — rotational invariance:**

For a signal of $K$ sinusoids, consider two overlapping "subarrays":

- $\mathbf{x}_1[n] = [x[n], x[n+1], ..., x[n+M-2]]^T$
- $\mathbf{x}_2[n] = [x[n+1], x[n+2], ..., x[n+M-1]]^T$

These differ by a single sample shift. The signal subspace of $\mathbf{x}_2$ is related to that of $\mathbf{x}_1$ by a "rotation" — multiplication by a diagonal matrix $\Phi$ whose eigenvalues are $e^{j2\pi f_k}$.

**The procedure:**

1. **Form the data matrix.** Stack $\mathbf{x}_1[n]$ and $\mathbf{x}_2[n]$ for many $n$.

2. **Compute the autocorrelation matrix** of the combined data.

3. **Eigendecomposition.** Identify the signal subspace ($K$ largest eigenvectors).

4. **Split the signal subspace into two parts** corresponding to the two subarrays: $U_{S1}$ and $U_{S2}$.

5. **Solve for $\Phi$:** find the matrix such that $U_{S2} = U_{S1} \Phi$ (in some optimal sense, typically least squares).

6. **Compute eigenvalues of $\Phi$:** they are $e^{j2\pi f_k}$. The frequencies are extracted directly.

**The rotational invariance:**

The trick is that the signal subspace itself doesn't depend on the absolute time origin — only on the frequencies. Shifting the data by one sample rotates each sinusoid's phase by $e^{j2\pi f_k}$, which is exactly what $\Phi$ encodes.

**Advantages over MUSIC:**

1. **No search.** MUSIC computes a pseudospectrum and finds peaks (a 1D search). ESPRIT directly solves for the frequencies — no peak finding.

2. **Faster.** No need to evaluate the pseudospectrum at many frequencies. Just one eigendecomposition + a least-squares solve.

3. **Direct frequency extraction.** The algorithm yields explicit frequency estimates, not just locations of peaks.

**Disadvantages:**

1. **Requires the rotational invariance structure.** Not as flexible as MUSIC.

2. **Sensitive to model order.** Like MUSIC, requires knowing $K$.

3. **Slightly less robust.** MUSIC's peak-finding can be more forgiving in some scenarios.

**Variants:**

- **TLS-ESPRIT (Total Least Squares):** more accurate than the original LS-ESPRIT.
- **Unitary ESPRIT:** uses real-valued matrices for better numerical stability.

**When to use ESPRIT vs MUSIC:**

- **ESPRIT:** when the data has the right structure (e.g., uniform sample spacing) and you want direct frequency extraction.
- **MUSIC:** when the data is more general or when the visualisation of a pseudospectrum is useful.

**Use cases:**

Same as MUSIC: DOA estimation, spectral analysis with super-resolution, system identification.

**Interview insight:** Knowing both MUSIC and ESPRIT shows breadth in subspace methods. Being able to articulate that ESPRIT is "MUSIC without the search" via rotational invariance shows understanding of the algorithmic structure.

### Q5. Compare parametric and non-parametric spectral estimation methods. When is each preferred?

**Answer:**

**Non-parametric methods** (FFT, periodogram, Welch, multitaper):

- Compute the spectrum directly from the data without assuming a model.
- The spectrum is whatever the data shows.
- Resolution limited by the data length (1/N for FFT).
- Robust to model misspecification.

**Parametric methods** (AR, MA, ARMA, MUSIC, ESPRIT):

- Assume a specific model for the signal.
- Estimate the model parameters from the data.
- Compute the spectrum from the model.
- Can have super-resolution if the model assumptions hold.
- Sensitive to model misspecification.

**When non-parametric is preferred:**

1. **General-purpose analysis.** When you don't know what's in the signal and want a "neutral" view.

2. **Long data.** If you have enough samples, the FFT is accurate and there's no need for parametric tricks.

3. **Broadband signals.** Signals with continuous spectra (noise, complex stochastic processes) don't fit narrow parametric models.

4. **Non-stationary signals** (with windowing). Parametric methods often assume stationarity.

5. **Robustness needed.** Non-parametric methods don't have model parameters to be wrong.

**When parametric is preferred:**

1. **Short data.** Parametric methods extract more information from limited samples.

2. **Sharp spectral peaks.** Sinusoids and resonances are well-modeled parametrically. Super-resolution is possible.

3. **Smooth output desired.** Parametric spectra are computed from continuous formulas — no leakage, no jaggedness.

4. **Specific signal structure.** If you know the signal is a sum of sinusoids, exploit that.

5. **Need to interpolate spectra.** Parametric models give continuous spectra; FFT only gives discrete bin values.

**Hybrid approaches:**

Some applications use both:

1. **Initial inspection with FFT.** Get a general view.
2. **Detailed analysis with parametric.** Once the structure is clear, use the appropriate parametric method.

**Computational comparison:**

| Method | Cost (rough) |
|---|---|
| FFT | $O(N \log N)$ |
| Welch | $O(N \log N)$ |
| Multitaper | $O(K N \log N)$ where $K$ is the number of tapers |
| AR (Burg) | $O(N p^2)$ |
| MUSIC | $O(M^3)$ for eigendecomposition + $O(M^2 F)$ for $F$ frequency points |
| ESPRIT | $O(M^3)$ |

For long data and many frequencies, FFT-based methods are the cheapest. For short data with sharp features, parametric is more efficient.

**Real-world choice:**

Almost all production signal analysis uses **non-parametric methods first**. Parametric methods are reserved for:

- Specific applications with known signal structure (radar, NMR, vibration).
- Research where super-resolution is the goal.
- Cases where the data is too short for FFT to give acceptable results.

**Interview insight:** A candidate who understands the trade-off (super-resolution at the cost of model assumptions) and can recommend the right tool for the workload shows mature spectral analysis judgement.

### Q6. Why does the periodogram have such high variance, and what techniques reduce it?

**Answer:**

The **periodogram** is the most basic spectral estimator:

$$\hat{S}(f) = \frac{1}{N} \left| \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] e^{-j2\pi fn} \right|^2$$

It has a notoriously bad property: its **variance does not decrease with N**. Even with infinite data, the periodogram's value at any frequency is randomly distributed around the true power.

**Why the variance is high:**

For a signal that's white noise of variance $\sigma^2$, the true spectrum is constant: $S(f) = \sigma^2$. But the periodogram values are approximately exponentially distributed with mean $\sigma^2$. The standard deviation equals the mean — 100% relative error.

Worse: the periodogram values at different frequencies are approximately independent. So a plot of the periodogram looks like noise around the true spectrum, not a smooth curve.

**Why averaging more samples doesn't help:**

You might think: "more samples → better estimate." But that's not how the periodogram works. As $N$ grows, the periodogram has more frequency bins, but each bin's variance stays the same. The total variance is spread over more bins, but each individual estimate is just as noisy.

The fundamental issue: the periodogram's estimator is not "sharing" information across frequencies. Each bin is its own independent (noisy) estimate.

**Variance reduction techniques:**

**1. Welch's method (averaged periodograms):**

Split the data into $K$ overlapping segments. Compute a periodogram for each. Average them.

$$\hat{S}_{Welch}(f) = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K \hat{S}_k(f)$$

If the periodograms are independent, averaging $K$ of them reduces variance by $1/K$. The catch: shorter segments mean lower frequency resolution. There's a trade-off.

**2. Smoothed periodogram (window averaging):**

Average the periodogram in a small frequency window:

$$\hat{S}_{smooth}(f) = \frac{1}{2M+1} \sum_{k=-M}^{M} \hat{S}(f + k \Delta f)$$

Reduces variance by $1/(2M+1)$ at the cost of frequency resolution.

**3. Multitaper:**

Use multiple orthogonal tapers (DPSS), compute periodograms with each, average. As discussed in Q1.

**4. Lag windowing (Blackman-Tukey):**

Compute the autocorrelation, multiply by a window in lag space, then Fourier transform. The window is chosen to weight short lags (well-estimated from data) more than long lags (poorly estimated from data).

**5. Parametric methods:**

AR, MUSIC, etc. — they exchange variance for model assumptions.

**Trade-off:**

All variance reduction techniques sacrifice **frequency resolution** for lower variance. The product of resolution × variance is roughly constant — you can't get both for free.

**Choosing the right technique:**

- **Long data, want overall variance reduction:** Welch with overlapping segments.
- **Need balanced variance and resolution:** multitaper.
- **Specific known structure:** parametric.
- **Quick first look:** raw periodogram (and just live with the noise).

**Interview insight:** A candidate who knows that the periodogram has constant variance (regardless of N) and can describe the techniques to fix it shows real spectral analysis depth. The "variance × resolution = constant" trade-off is the key insight.
